Catherine McDermott

I'm currently writing a monograph based on my PhD research, which studiedÂ how 1990s/early 2000s postfeminist discourses of female empowerment shaped contemporary media aimed at women.

My work analyses how pop culture navigates the contradictory effects that growing up during this time period had on female identity.

I found that in narratives about women in their late twenties and early thirties like Gone Girl and Girls, there was an overwhelming sense of loss, disappointment and failure. Protagonists like Amy Dunne and Hannah Horvath who have grown up on a diet of Sex and the City come to realise that consumerism and sexual liberation is not delivering the promise of happiness or fulfilment.

This contrasts with coming-of-age stories like The Hunger Games, where resilient young women are seen as capable of overcoming and adapting to much harsher dystopian conditions.

The aim of my research was to map and understand these contrasts, and to explore the ongoing legacy of postfeminism.

I teach Critical and Contextual Studies at University of Salford and Critical and Cultural Theory at Manchester Metropolitan University.

For the past year I worked with FACT Liverpool as an AHRC-funded Researcher & Writer in Residence, contributing to The Future World of Work project and partnering with the Learning Team to create research-led workshops on gender and sexuality for young people.
